Offer

Compensation details


Theory

This idea merits its own section because it's the foundation of any offer. Because compensation is so tied to necessities, if this is a no-go, no number of "nice to haves" are likely to make up for it.

Every offer will outlined your proposed salary, any bonuses, retirement contributions, health and wellness packages, and perks. Also, there will be a clear expectation of other things affecting you monetarily, like a commute, dress code, or work travel.

Evaluating these areas against your current situation is a critical step to properly evaluate an offer.

In summary: see your compensation not as just salary, but anything that affects you monetarily.


Practice

First, start with your current job. Outline the categories of compensation and assign a figure or short description (i.e. $80K/yr, 5% 401K match, $350/mo premium, etc). Next, match each of your offers by category against your current situation with similar numbers/descriptions.

You might envision a table like this for a "produce manager" at a grocery store:

Category Walmart (current) Target (offer) Smith's (offer)
Salary $65K/yr $70K/yr $72K/yr
Bonuses $5K/yr $3K/yr $3K/yr
Retirement contrib 3% instant, up to 7% match 0% instant, up to 5% match 3% instant, no match
Health insurance $250/mo | $3,000 deduct $400/mo | $3,500 deduct $300/mo | $2,200 deduct
Commute exp $1,700/yr $200/yr $500/yr
... ... ... ...

Don't overlook even the smallest detail. For some, expensive suits, watches, briefcase, and a nice car approach basic necessities. Those are recurring costs and you need to account for them.

With such an outline, you could accurately compare and adjust for what matters/doesn't matter to you.


Application

  1. Get detailed when comparing compensation (including potential for additional compensation and non-reimbursed expenses)
