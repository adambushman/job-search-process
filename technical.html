Interviews

Technical


Theory

The whole idea behind technical interviews is bridging the knowledge gap companies have for what a candidate says they can do and what a candidate can actually do. This is their biggest risk in the whole process!

Not every industry or profession requires a technical interview but all of them do require you to know how to do the job. When seen from this angle, there should be less fear if you know your stuff.

In summary: this is a grand opportunity to show your how, the way you work differently from others; embrace it.


Practice

During the assets section, it was suggested you develop a portfolio to showcase your work. If you've taken this route, a technical interview shouldn't be scary because you already 1) know your stuff and 2) should you make mistakes, you have a primary reference for other great work.

If you don't have a portfolio and are shy about a technical interview, it may be an indication that you 1) don't know your stuff as well as you think and/or 2) aren't comfortable presenting/demonstrating. If either of these describe you, do some upskill and practice.

Also, there's a good many books with industry specific technical interview preparation advice. Try a google search!

Furthermore, if you use Excel a lot or PowerPoint, chances are those in the industry do as well. Or if the job specifically states proficiency with Adobe Illustrator, that's a good hint of what may be in a technical interview.


Application

  1. If you're sufficiently prepared, you needn't fear; if you're unprepared, change your expectations to a learning opportunity
  2. Marry the content (which they already know about) with your who, what, and how; that's what they REALLY care about
