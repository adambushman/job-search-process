Who/What

Who you are


Theory

This may sound corny but it's far more serious that it may seem: who are you?

The idea here is that you'll have a hard time advocating for yourself on the job market if you can't describe you you are. Let's be clear, who you are IS NOT what you do. Some will cite their profession or job title, but that's more of what they do, not who they are.

Who you are is more about what you believe, what you're passionate about, and how you're committed to doing things. It can be pretty abstract.

Let's drive this point home with a couple examples:

Steve Jobs

Who was Steve Jobs? Certainly "what" he did was entrepreneurship, he was CEO of Apple, and inspired world changing inventions. But does that really describe who he was?

Perhaps we can learn how he viewed himself with how he described Apple:

Steve Jobs was an "out-of-the-box" thinker, passionate about consumer experience, and committed to quality and excellence.

Mother Theresa

Who was Mother Theresa? Most would describe her Catholic faith, her generous philanthropic and charitable work. Is that who she was or the expression of who she was?

This quote, among many, may clarify:

Mother Theresa was a leader and an example, passionate about changing the world, and commited to doing so person by person with many small actions.


In summary: separate who you are from what you do.


Practice

Steve Jobs, being an "out-of-the-box" thinker, passionate about consumer experience, and committed to qulaity and excellence, could have chosen any industry and still felt fulfilled. Perhaps, Mother Theresa could have carried out that mission as the head of a non-profit, in lieu of her ministry.

The point is that job fulfillment and success is more a product of who you are than what you do. You can change what you do but it's difficult and likely counter productive to change who you are.

Get abstract! Write down qualities, thoughts, passions, commitments. Talk with those close to you for inspiration. The time is worth the effort.


Application

  1. Separate who you are from what you do; think about who you are personally and professionally irrespective of job title, industry, etc.
