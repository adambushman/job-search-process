Why

Must haves/nice to haves


Theory

Stephen R. Covey, author of 7 Habits of Highly Effective People often shared the principle of prioritization with an example of a jar one must fill with rocks, gravel, and sand.

Were you to begin filling the jar with items small to big, you'd find the contents unable to fit. Ordering big to small, however, is a different matter.

When job offers inevitably come your way toward the end of the process, odds are not every item on your ordered list of motivations will be met. You've already prioritized what you're after but now it's time to get MORE specific. It will become your own shortcut for decision making along the way.

In summary: ensure your "musts" end up truly being "haves".


Practice

Here's the thing about ordered lists: they give the impression that the gap in value from one to the next is the same.

One approach to correct this weakness is to separate items into tiers where the value gap between items within a tier is similar. For our purposes, let's keep it simple. Create two tiers: 1) "Must haves" and 2) "Nice to haves".

Your "must haves" are items on which you won't compromise. It also helps to get specific for each item in this tier. For example, if you've identified "higher salary" as a must have, work to zero in on a specific threshold.

Your "nice to haves" are items that may be forgone should circumstances throughout the hiring process makes you passionate about the opportunity.


Application

  1. Separate your prioritized list of motivations into tiers: 1) "must haves" and 2) "nice to haves"
  2. Consider specifics for each of your "must haves"
