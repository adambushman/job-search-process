Check-in #3

Iteration


Theory

You're doing it! You're getting those interviews and be exposed to real people, real questions, real problems. Thse are the best teachers in the process!

If you haven't figured it out yet, here's a little secrete: you probably suck at first. This isn't a you problem, it's a human problem. With few exceptions, doing something new for the first time is a humbling experience.

Your first interview you probably were nervous and stumbled over your words (I felt that way). You may have bombed your first technical interview (I did). You may have been ghosted, left wondering if the company skill exists (I did as well).

Don't fret! It's part of the process. It's practice! All of this material is to give you the best, inexperienced leg to stand on as you get your feet wet in the process.

Now, it's about iterating: taking the feedback from each subsequent event to inform and refine your next event.

In summary: iteration is how every quality aspect of your life was achieved.


Practice

Here's some examples of the feedback I got along the way from good and bad events, and what I did to make the next event better:

  • I bombed my first technical interview. I was unfamiliar with some advanced topics of a data skill called SQL.
    • I found a free, online tool to help learn and practice SQL
    • I spent probably 50 hours learning and practicing
    • I documented my work and showcased it in future interviews
  • I had a good interview but it was clear I was underqualified for the position (they were filling a generalized role where I was more suited to that of a specialized)
    • I shied away from applying to jobs that required that breadth of knowledge and experience
    • I tailored my content to emphasize my specialized abilities
  • I failed the question of "tell us about yourself" early on. I realized it was a golden opportunity to summarize what made me different.
    • I created a memorized summary for this question
    • I interweaved who, what, how with my education, experience, and projects, demonstrating value and peaking their interest
    • I noticed the change immediately as most interviews built upon that first introduction

Application

  1. Listen to the market; it'll tell you where you are off, and if you experiment, you'll settle on a winning formula.
