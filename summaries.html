Summaries


Theory

Remember when you were engaged in conversation with an associate and their anecdote seemed to drag on? Instead of succinctly leading you through the events with an engaging narrative, they seemed determined to lengthen the story and fixate upon irrelevant details?

This is common to most, save seasoned orators and naturally matter of fact persons. The consequence of droning on is a disengaged audience. That is the last thing you want in an interview!

Summaries are a way to get out in front of this pitfall and learn more toward the "seasoned orator" end of the spectrum.

In summary: people love good stories, but good stories are carefully crafted.


Practice

A majority of interviews follow a similar pattern. The same questions are generally asked and you can be ready for them. In fact, a perfectly crafted summary for each is your best route.

At minimum, come up with 1 minute blurbs you can memorize for each of these questions:

  • "Tell us about yourself"
    • This IS NOT an invitation to talk for 30 mins about your love for animals or your irrelevant hobbies.
    • This is a golden opportunity to set the tone and direction of the interview.
    • Work to weave your why, how, and what in with specific, unique details.
    • End with your interest to provide similar value for the prospective company.
  • "What makes you unique?"
    • Don't hesitate to be a broken record. Recycle any content but present it differently.
    • Stress your "how" and get to specific projects, highlights, etc.
  • "Tell me about a time when..."
    • These questions come in a myriad of flavors. "...overcame a challenge", "...disagreed with a coworker", etc.
    • You can't anticipate all of these by perhaps write out some powerful anecdotes that could be altered slightly to fit the exact question.
    • With any question of this sort, try to stick to the STAR format:
      1. "S"ituation
      2. "T"ask
      3. "A"ction
      4. "R"esult

Remember, only a minute (that's about 140 words). This forces you to be succint and only say what matters to them. Always tie back to the content you've been creating thus far.


Application

  1. Outline 1 min summaries for popular interview questions and memorize them.
