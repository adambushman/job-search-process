Why

Start with why


Theory

Motivations are critical to anything worthwhile. Stop and think for a second about the grandest things you've done, what you're most proud of. Do you have a clear sense for what motivated that seismic effort? I'll bet you do.

Getting in touch with why you're looking to shake up your career will guide much of the details of your search. Without a clear picture, you'll likely find yourself falling short of the destination you've envisioned for yourself.

We aren't used to tapping into why for our decisions. Most choices are whimsical, spur of the moment. Hiuristics (mental shortcuts) help reduce mental computation. None of these descriptions align with taking control of your career.

In summary: take the time to outline your motivations.


Practice

People change careers for all sorts of reasons. Some are worthwhile and sound, while, frankly, others are misguided. Certainly all are best intentioned but not all align with the desired outcome. Review the below hotlist of motivations, taking a mental note of which best align with your own:

  • Pay
  • Commute
  • Benefits
  • Opportunities
  • Work type
  • Industry
  • Coworkers
  • Leadership
  • Mentorship
  • Etc

You may have your eye on a handful of items from the list but certainly you've already done some mental gymnastics to put them in a kind of hierarchy (prioritizing some over others).

An ordered list will be a critical reference for the rest of your job search. You'll find this list will eliminate jobs for which you might otherwise apply. You may find yourself even turning down offers for not meeting your why.

Even now, at this early stage, I encourage you to challenge your list for any items that don't mix (or might appear contradictory). A couple examples:

  • A warehouse associate with the intention of changing jobs to become 100% remote should also have listed a change of industry
  • An aspiring climate activist who lists high pay as a motivation should be skeptical; gratification in this career will most likely come via improved self-worth or recognition, and not via wealth

Application

  1. Compile a prioritized list of motivations for changing jobs; feel free to use a single word/phrase title and write descriptions
  2. Scrutinize your list for incongruities; it's important to be honest with yourself about both sides of the coin (what's happening with you and what's happening out there)
