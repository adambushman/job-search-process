Introductory details


Theory

Having thoroughly explored your motivations for pursing a new professional opportunity, dissected who you are, what you do, and how you do it, why is this section necessary? That's best answered with an analogy.

Fishing is a popular past time. Even if all your exposure has been passing through the fishing section of an outdoors store, you likely know there's more to the hobby and sport that line + hook.

But when you think about it, those are the only essentials you really need. The fancy rod, convenient tackle box, nifty gear, shiny lures, and multicolored bait are, literally and figuratively, window dressing.

The real meat and potatoes of your job search hinge on what you've already outlined, but the window dressing is how you attract enough fish to land a bite.

In summary: start getting comfortable with the idea of selling the idea of yourself as an employee to potential employers.


Practice

So what is all this window dressing. Well, it's much of the introductory information about you that first peaks an employer's interest. These often include:

  • Personal info
    • Contact info: name, photo, phone, email, address
    • Links: social media, portfolio
    • Hobbies/interests
  • Experience
    • Professional Title: current, seeking, or both
    • Work history: company, title, timeline
    • Education history: organization, degree, timeline
  • Abilities
    • Power statements: short blurbs about value created
    • Projects: a way to publicly demonstrate your value
    • Skills: hard and soft, relevant to the industry
    • Awards or certifications

This is all the traditional info you've likely already compiled for a resume in the past.

Let's get it all into a single place. Don't worry about formatting, just make sure the information is organized and generally descriptive. For each category, list keywords (single words or short phrases that encapsulate the idea).

For instance, instead of citing "Amazon" as work experience, I'd instead say "Amazon, 12/1/20 - 10/9/23, shift manager". This isn't a short form document; it's a master reference, a backbone for the rest of your job search.

Let's dive into two of the above bullets in greater detail:

Power Statements

These are more than a list of responsibilities you handle on a day-to-day basis. They're also not even concerned with what you've done in school or work previously. This is the place to communicate value.

Value is key, but there's three additional rules: 1) short sentance, 2) begin with an action word, and 3) specific.

Assuming I work in the supply chain industry, here's a bad and good example of a powerstatement

I am responsible for managing inventory levels for the company and supporting sales and marketing functions with stock.

Ensures optimal inventory for 250 products and 3 locations, leading to the #1 year of sales.

Projects

The biggest challenge for companies in the interview process is reconciling what little they know of the candidate with what they require for the job. Just because a candidate says they can build mobile apps doesn't mean they align with the company's requirements.

One of the best ways to stand out is to offer a path to evaluate your work. Projects should be a public demonstration you 1) can do the job and 2) bring unique value.

Either receive permission from to share your past professional work with the public or start a project on your personal time that is tangential to your work. Document everything you can, including pictures, diagrams, and documents.

I'm comfortable with large, corporate sales negotiation.

I negotiated a $200M sales partnership with Ford. Here's a blog post detailing the process and result with photos.


A word of caution: be careful not to misrepresent yourself. It'll come back to haunt you.


Application

  1. Compose a master reference of personal info, experience, and abilities.
  2. Keep it long form, raw, and detailed. We'll polish it up based on the delivery system we use.
